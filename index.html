Hi, I'm Carter! I research how people make moral judgments and statistical inferences in the Effective Altruism Psychology Lab at New York University. I previously researched social learning in the Moral Psychology Research Lab at Harvard University. Broadly, my research aims to understand how people form moral judgments judgments and make altruistic decisions under conditions of ethical and empirical uncertainty. How do people decide what to do when they're not sure what's right, and/or when they don't know what effect their action will have, anyway?

I enjoy thinking about a bunch of dorky topics, but recently I'm especially interested in group decision-making, Bayesian reasoning, and existential risk (especially why people aren't more freaked out about the fact that humanity might go extinct this century, and what those of us who are appropriately freaked out can do).

Although I think a lot about stupidity and death, I also try to be a fun person! I love meeting new people and try to keep an autobiographical meet-me document updated here. I also longboard, occasionally write, and make really terrible computer music in my free time. Once in a while, I make a mistake, and you can give me anonymous feedback about how to make less of those here.

I built this website myself, by which I mean that I hardly did anything and outsourced 80% of the coding to ChatGPT, which is how I do about 15% of my job these days, too. Soon, I'll update this page with more academic stuff (like my CV, research projects) and any writing or papers I publish.
